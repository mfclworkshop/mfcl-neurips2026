Mathematical Foundations of Continual Learning

Continual learning beyond benchmarks: towards a foundational theory of adaptive learning systems

NeurIPS 2026 Workshop Proposal Sydney, Australia
Why this workshop?

Workshop Motivation

The study of continual adaptation spans many communities, but its core concepts remain fragmented across different languages, assumptions, and mathematical frameworks.

01

Static Training

Most learning theory still begins with fixed datasets, fixed objectives, and fixed evaluation distributions.

02

Dynamic Worlds

Deployed systems face shifting data, changing tasks, new feedback, and evolving needs.

03

Missing Foundations

Concepts such as plasticity, forgetting, and transfer remain difficult to define precisely.

Central Question

How can continual learning move beyond benchmark-driven progress toward a foundational theory of adaptation?

Modern machine learning faces a fundamental disconnect between how models are trained and how they are deployed. State-of-the-art systems are typically trained on static datasets, yet real-world environments evolve continuously (Parisi et al., 2018). As a result, deployed models must continually adapt to new information without compromising previously learned capabilities (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Thrun, 1998).

Despite recent empirical progress in continual learning, the principles governing sustained adaptation remain poorly understood. In practice, progress in the field has been driven by empirical performance improvements (Van de Ven et al., 2022) more than agreed-upon scientific principles. Consequently, concepts such as memory, transfer, and forgetting are often used colloquially and lack precise definitions, leading to inconsistent interpretations and hindering effective communication between researchers

Establishing the mathematical foundations of adaptation is therefore essential. Beyond improving algorithms, theoretical foundations help formalize intuitions, clarify assumptions, and establish a shared language for reasoning about adaptation (Abel et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2025; Sanati et al., 2026). This workshop aims to advance this effort by bringing together researchers from continual learning, online learning, reinforcement learning, meta-learning, optimization, and related areas to develop shared perspectives on the principles of continual adaptation.

Tracks and Review

The main track is intended for more complete theoretical, empirical, or conceptual contributions. The tiny track is intended for early-stage ideas, open problems, negative results, speculative theoretical directions, or position papers intended to stimulate discussion.

Main Track

5-page submissions, excluding references and appendices.

Tiny Track

2-page submissions, excluding references and appendices.

Review Criteria
  • • Relevance to the workshop themes and goals
  • • Technical soundness, rigor, and clarity
  • • Originality and impact of the contribution
  • • Potential to stimulate cross-community discussion and future research

Workshop Research Directions

Theoretical Foundations

Theoretical analyses of sequential adaptation in deep learning systems, including but not limited to optimization, features and representations, architectures, memory, and data.

Concepts & Assumptions

Work that clarifies core concepts, assumptions, or definitions underlying continual learning; proposes new problem formulations; or critically examines commonly accepted paradigms and evaluation methodologies.

Learning Dynamics

Studies of learning dynamics, including phenomena such as catastrophic forgetting, loss of plasticity, stability-plasticity trade-offs, interference, transfer and long-horizon adaptation.

Unifying Paradigms

Theories and frameworks that connect continual learning across applications and settings, highlighting shared mathematical structure between online learning, reinforcement learning, test-time adaptation, meta-learning, and other relevant fields.
