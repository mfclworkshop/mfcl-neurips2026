Mathematical Foundations of Continual Learning

Bridging the gap between static optimization and lifelong adaptation.

NeurIPS 2026 Workshop Proposal Sydney, Australia

Workshop Motivation

Modern machine learning faces a disconnect between how models are trained and how they are deployed. Today's most capable models are typically trained on static datasets, yet they must operate in dynamic, continually evolving environments (Thrun, 1998; Ring, 1994). This discrepancy creates an urgent need for learning algorithms that can adapt continually across a variety of settings. Because of this widespread demand, research on continual adaptation has become fragmented across multiple communities, including supervised continual learning, continual reinforcement learning, online learning, test-time adaptation, in-context learning, and meta-learning, each with its own terminology, benchmarks, and evaluation protocols (see Wang et al., 2024, for a recent survey).

The absence of a unified mathematical foundation of adaptation has left many of its fundamental aspects insufficiently formalized. Core concepts such as memory, transfer, and forgetting often lack precise definitions, leading to frequent misinterpretation and hindering effective communication across communities (Abel et al., 2023; Sanati et al., 2025). Consequently, benchmark-driven evaluation has become the primary indicator of progress (Van de Ven et al., 2022), leaving the foundations of adaptation underexplored.

We believe that building systems that are natively adaptive requires more than incremental work on foundations designed for static settings—it requires treating adaptivity as a first principle rather than a fix. A more expressive mathematical language is needed to define objectives, evaluate behaviour, and compare methods, e.g., beyond the in-distribution / out-of-distribution dichotomy. Such foundations would provide conceptual clarity, unify research fields that are currently disconnected, and translate into practical gains in how continual learning methods are designed and evaluated. This workshop aims to advance this effort by bringing together researchers across machine learning to discuss the foundations of continual learning, supporting a shift from benchmark-driven evaluation toward a more rigorous scientific understanding of continual adaptation .

Panel & Oxford-Style Debate

The workshop will conclude with an Oxford-style debate centered on a major open question in continual learning and adaptive systems. The goal is to move beyond benchmark-driven discussion and create a space for contrasting theoretical perspectives on what the field should prioritize next.

The debate will bring together researchers from online learning, reinforcement learning, optimization, Bayesian inference, and deep learning theory to discuss competing views on the mathematical foundations of continual learning.
