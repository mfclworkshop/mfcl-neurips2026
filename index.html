Mathematical Foundations of Continual Learning

Bridging the gap between static optimization and lifelong adaptation.

NeurIPS 2026 Workshop Proposal Sydney, Australia

Workshop Motivation

While modern foundation models excel on static, i.i.d. datasets, real-world deployment demands adaptivity at scale. Standard optimization algorithms often fail over long horizons, suffering from catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity (Sanati et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025; Lyle et al., 2024; Dohare et al., 2024).

MFCL 2026 shifts the focus from "benchmark-chasing" to thinking about mathematical foundations. We bring together experts from optimization, deep learning theory, and statistical learning to build a shared theoretical language for sequential learning.

Panel & Oxford-Style Debate

The workshop will conclude with an Oxford-style debate centered on a major open question in continual learning and adaptive systems. The goal is to move beyond benchmark-driven discussion and create a space for contrasting theoretical perspectives on what the field should prioritize next.

The debate will bring together researchers from online learning, reinforcement learning, optimization, Bayesian inference, and deep learning theory to discuss competing views on the mathematical foundations of continual learning.

Call for Papers

Submissions Due: August 29, 2026 AoE

Accept/Reject Notification Date: September 29, 2026, AoE

Workshop Date: December 11 and December 12, 2026 (Sydney)

  • Conceptual Foundations: Clarifying core assumptions, definitions, and abstractions underlying continual and sequential learning.
  • Unifying Perspectives: Frameworks that bridge online learning, reinforcement learning, Bayesian inference, and deep learning theory.
  • Benchmarks & Assumptions: Critical examination of evaluation protocols, dataset assumptions, and experimental practices in continual learning.
  • Adaptive Systems: Understanding plasticity, memory, stability, and long-horizon adaptation in large-scale neural systems.
